## Where Our Programming Comes From

Our thoughts and feelings, and the beliefs they form, are shaped by everything we have experienced.

Our environment, our upbringing, trauma, chronic stress, relationships, periods of illness or infection, and the world we have been exposed to through media, friends, and culture.

Most of this programming happened before we were old enough to consciously choose it.

---

## The Two Critical Windows

**0–7 years**

Between the ages of zero and seven, our brain was predominantly in theta brainwave state. The same state adults enter during deep hypnosis or meditation.

There was essentially no critical filter.

Everything went straight in.

Everything we saw, heard, felt, and experienced was recorded as truth and became our operating programme.

This is where our deepest beliefs about ourselves, about safety, about worthiness, and about what is possible for us were laid down. We were not choosing these beliefs.

They were being downloaded.

**7–14 years**

Between seven and fourteen, our brain shifted into alpha. Still highly impressionable, but a critical filter began to develop.

Our identity, our social beliefs, and the stories about who we are in relation to others were reinforced here.

Peer influence, school, and early experiences of success and failure all played a role.

**The implication:** By the time we reached adulthood, we were already running a programme that was largely written before we were old enough or conscious enough to choose it.

And we have been confirming it ever since.

## How Our Beliefs Were Formed

Thoughts linked with consistent feelings create beliefs.

When we repeatedly think the same thought and pair it with the same emotional response, it gets wired in as a belief.

Do it enough times and it becomes automatic.

Our body starts producing the emotional response before the conscious thought even arrives.

At that point it is no longer something we are choosing to believe.

It is a programme running silently in the background.

By the time we are adults, 95% of our thoughts and feelings are unconscious.

Beliefs running beneath our awareness, quietly shaping how we see ourselves, what we expect from life, and how our body functions — and what we create — day to day.

## What Trauma Does

Ordinary repetition builds beliefs slowly.

Trauma builds them instantly.

And buries them deep.

When something overwhelming happened to us, our nervous system went into emergency mode.

An accident, a loss, abuse, a shocking diagnosis, a prolonged period of chronic stress.

The experience was too much to process in the moment.

So instead of filing it, our body froze it.

It captured an emotional snapshot of that moment and stored it unprocessed in our nervous system.

That frozen emotional state stayed live.

Our subconscious has been treating that unprocessed experience as an unresolved threat ever since. Something still requiring a response.

The rule is this: the stronger the emotion, the faster and deeper the belief gets wired.

A single traumatic experience, felt with enough intensity, can install a belief that would otherwise take thousands of repetitions to form.

And because that emotional snapshot is stored in the body rather than the conscious mind, it keeps reactivating.

Our nervous system scans constantly for anything resembling the original threat.

When it finds a match, even a partial one, it fires the same response as if the original event is happening right now.

This is why trauma responses can feel disproportionate to what is in front of us. We are not responding to the present moment. We are responding to a memory our body never finished processing.

## The Chemistry of What We Feel

There is an associated chemistry with every thought and every corresponding emotion we feel.

Some thoughts and emotions push our body into survival mode.

They activate the stress response, flood our system with cortisol and adrenaline, and keep our body on high alert.

These are the emotions like: fear, anger, guilt, irritability, impatience, apathy, shame, grief, and despair.

Other thoughts and emotions recharge, repair, and support our body.

They produce oxytocin, DHEA, serotonin, and dopamine.

These are the chemical responses associated with emotions like gratitude, appreciation, love, and care.

Here is what that looks like in practice.

We have the unconscious thought: "I hate this."

That thought produces the corresponding emotion: anger, felt in the body.

Our heart rate speeds up. Our breathing shortens. There is more tension in our jaw, our chest, our shoulders.

And alongside all of that, our body produces the chemistry of anger.

Cortisol. Adrenaline.

The full stress response.

If we are unconsciously feeling angry — not occasionally, but as a baseline state running in the background — our body is consistently living from a state of survival.

And a body in survival mode is not a body that can heal. Or create.

This is not a theory. It is measurable.

Research from the HeartMath Institute found that people who spent just five minutes feeling anger showed suppressed immune function for up to five hours afterwards.

Specifically, a frontline immune marker called secretory IgA dropped significantly and stayed low.

People who spent five minutes feeling appreciation showed elevated IgA for up to six hours.

One emotional state. One five-minute experience. Six hours of measurable impact on the immune system.

The same research showed that generating sustained elevated emotional states like appreciation and care significantly increased DHEA levels in the blood.

DHEA is the body's primary anti-ageing hormone. It counteracts cortisol, supports cellular repair, and strengthens immune function.

Chronic stress also shortens telomeres — the protective caps on our DNA that determine how quickly our cells age and deteriorate. This was the basis of Nobel Prize-winning research by Elizabeth Blackburn.

Positive emotional states and consistent meditation practice have been shown to slow and in some cases reverse that deterioration.

And heart rate variability — one of the most reliable markers of nervous system health — measurably increases when the body feels elevated emotional states.

Low heart rate variability is directly associated with anxiety, chronic illness, and poor recovery.

High heart rate variability is associated with resilience, repair, and health.

Our body responds to what we feel. Not occasionally. Consistently. At the cellular level.

## What Fight/Flight Does to the Brain

When the nervous system is locked in fight/flight, the brain shifts into high beta brainwaves.

High beta is a state of hypervigilance. The brain is scanning constantly for threats, problems, and dangers — most of which are not actually present.

In this state:
- Overthinking is unavoidable. The brain is doing exactly what it is designed to do in survival mode.
- Elevated emotions like joy, freedom, love, and gratitude become inaccessible. The nervous system is producing survival chemistry, not coherence chemistry.
- The alpha and theta brainwave states — associated with creativity, flow, deep relaxation, and subconscious access — are blocked by the high beta dominance.
- The heart effectively closes off. There is no pathway to generate or sustain the elevated emotional states required for subconscious reprogramming.

This is why people who understand conscious creation completely still cannot make it work.

You cannot reprogram the subconscious from the head.

The subconscious mind does not respond to information or intellectual understanding. It responds to feeling — to the consistent, repeated, felt experience of being the version of yourself who already has what you are trying to create.

And that felt experience is inaccessible when you are in survival mode.

**The heart's own nervous system:** The heart contains approximately 40,000 neurons — enough to function as its own independent brain. HeartMath researchers call it the "little brain" of the heart. It can sense, feel, learn and remember independently of the brain.

**The heart leads the brain:** The heart sends more signals to the brain than the brain sends to the heart. The heart's afferent signals directly influence the brain's higher cognitive functions — perception, decision-making, emotional processing.

**The electromagnetic field:** The heart generates the body's most powerful electromagnetic field — measurable several feet outside the body. It is approximately 60 times greater in amplitude than the electrical field of the brain.

**Sacred and ancient meaning:** Every major culture and spiritual tradition — Egyptian, Greek, Hebrew, Christian, Islamic, Hindu, Buddhist — placed the heart at the centre of human consciousness, wisdom and spiritual experience. The idea that intelligence lives in the head is a relatively recent Western development. The world's oldest traditions knew otherwise.

**The misconception reframe:** "Logic lives in the head, emotion lives in the heart — and logic is stronger." This is the dominant cultural belief. It is also precisely why so many people are stuck. They are trying to create their life from the weaker of the two organs. Getting into the heart is not becoming more emotional. It is accessing a more intelligent, more coherent way of operating.

## 4. Voice and Structure Rules

### The writing style — non-negotiable

- **Short lines.** Single sentences as their own paragraph. Creates pace and pulls the reader down.
- **Ellipsis (...)** used deliberately to create forward momentum and open loops. Not overused.
- **Plain language throughout.** No clinical terms, no wellness jargon, no corporate language.
- **British English** — spelling, grammar, phrasing throughout.
- **First person, conversational** — sounds like a message from someone who has been through it.
- **Never preachy.** Never "you deserve this." Never aspirational wellness-influencer tone.
- **Personal story is the primary credibility anchor** — not credentials.
- **Rhetorical questions** used to maintain momentum.
- **Reframe blame before offering solution** — "it's not your fault" and "it's not an information problem" remove self-blame and open the prospect to the real cause.
- **Manage expectations honestly** — "it is going to take learning and consistent practice" builds credibility.
- **Specific losses and specific life events** outperform vague statements — name the wedding, the sport, the business.
- **Write like you talk** — casual, personality-driven, imperfect beats polished and corporate (see Section 8).

### Subject lines
- Mirror the first line or emotional hook of the email
- 5–9 words
- Plain language — no clickbait, no false urgency
- Always use one of the 13 techniques in Section 8 — rotate, never repeat the same technique twice in a row
- Examples: *"This is not your fault"* / *"Why it keeps crashing back"* / *"I know you've tried everything"* / *"The missing piece isn't more information"*

### Preview text
- Continues the subject line's thought without repeating it or giving away the punchline
- Should read as one continuous hook alongside the subject line, not a restatement
- Keep short — roughly 40–90 characters

### Length
- **150–300 words** — every line earns its place
- Longer only if a personal story or framework genuinely requires it (max 400 words)
- Ben Settle's sweet spot is 200–300 words, ideal around 250 — lean toward the shorter end for broadcast emails specifically

### Emoji usage
- Minimal and purposeful — 0 to 2 per email
- 👉 to open a key statement
- ✅ for Promised Land / positive outcomes only
- ➡️ or ⏩ for symptom or problem lists
- 1️⃣2️⃣3️⃣ for numbered frameworks only
- 💚 occasionally at the end of a personal or mission email — sparingly
- Never decorative

### Lists
- ✅ checkmarks — Promised Land, positive outcomes
- ➡️ arrows — symptoms, problems, what they're experiencing
- 1️⃣2️⃣3️⃣ — sequential frameworks only
- 4–6 items maximum

## 5. CTA Options

Choose one per email — or use no CTA for empathy and open-loop emails.

| CTA Type | When to use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Join Community link** | Conviction and invitation emails (late nurture sequence) | "If you'd like some help putting this into practice — you can join the Heart Creator Community here: [link]" |
| **Open loop** | When the concept needs to land and sit | End on the idea. Let it breathe. No link. |
| **Reply invite** | Empathy and connection emails, or survey-style emails | "Drop me a reply if any of this resonates — I read every one." |
| **No CTA** | Pure empathy and personal story emails | End on a personal commitment or reassurance. No ask. |

**Join Community link:** `https://www.skool.com/heartcreator`

**CTA tone rules:**
- Always soft and conversational — never urgent, never pushy
- Never "click here" or "sign up now"
- Frame it as an offer, not an instruction
- "If you'd like some help with this" is a proven soft lead-in

### CTA Format — Non-Negotiable

CTAs are always inline hyperlinked text, never a plain URL and never `===> HERE`:

Approved:
```
If you'd like some help putting this into practice, you can join the Heart Creator Community here.
[here links to: https://www.skool.com/heartcreator?utm_source=email&utm_medium=broadcast&utm_campaign=community]
```

Rejected:
```
You can join the Heart Creator Community ===> HERE
```
Why rejected: `===> HERE` reads as over the top — hyperlink the natural word in the sentence instead.

**CTA frequency (broadcast):** the majority of broadcast emails carry no CTA. Use the Heart Creator Community CTA roughly every 7th email — check recent sends in Notion before adding one so it doesn't feel like a constant pitch.

Every channel gets its own UTM. Never copy an Instagram UTM into an email.

## 8. Email Player Secrets (Ben Settle)

> Source: "The Email Player's Playbook" by Ben Settle (Copyright 2010-2016, www.BenSettle.com), extracted from screenshots Olly provided 2026-07-03. **Not gospel** — a toolbox to make hooks, subject lines and content ideas sharper. Always adapt through Heart Creator's brand voice; never copy Ben Settle's raw tone or crude language. **This section applies by default to every email drafted, not only when asked** — confirmed as standing instruction 2026-07-03.

### 8.1 The Core Philosophy

**"Infotainment," not hard teaching.** An email should be about 90% entertainment, 10% information. Soft teaching (tell them WHAT to do and why, not HOW) beats hard teaching (a full how-to) almost every time — hard teaching satisfies people so they don't buy; soft teaching gets them curious enough to want the full answer.

- **Don't hard teach.** Give a real insight, but leave the "how" for the product/offer. Analogy: "a hotdog that nourishes you like broccoli" — looks fun, still nourishes.
- **Bait the hook, don't feed the whole fish.** Give enough to get their interest, not the whole meal.
- **Write like you talk.** Casual, personality-driven, imperfect. "Un-okay" people are more persuasive than polished ones. This already matches Olly's brand voice rule of sounding human, not corporate.
- **Boredom is the real enemy**, not being disliked. Mix and match formats so no single type gets stale (Ben's Star Trek Borg analogy — don't overuse even your best trick).

Heart Creator's brand voice already bans hard-sell hype and corporate tone, so infotainment fits naturally. The adaptation is to keep every technique inside British English, no em-dashes, no crude language, and soft/conversational CTAs — Ben's own style is much more brash and "always be closing" than Olly's; borrow the mechanics, not the mouth.

### 8.2 Email Content Types (rotate for variety)

These map onto our existing Type of Email options (Belief-Changing, Personal Story, Personal Mission, Case Study, Engagement, Personal Update) — use these angles to vary the *within-type* approach so the list never gets bored of the format:

| Type | What it is | Heart Creator angle |
|---|---|---|
| **Story** | A real event, twisted into a reason to buy/act. Doesn't have to be dramatic — a flat tire, a haircut, dropping the kids off. "Nothing bad ever happens to an email marketer — it's all material." | Mine smaller everyday moments too, not just the two big stories (chronic illness, $13k month) |
| **Q&A** | Answer a real (or plausible) question from a subscriber/prospect. Builds dialogue, never hard-teaches the full answer. | Answer a real objection from `prospect_intel.md` in Q&A form |
| **Checklist** | "X ways to..." or "X mistakes people make with...". Easy to write when low on energy, still valuable. | "5 things keeping you stuck in your head instead of your heart" etc. |
| **Inspirational** | Challenge the reader to act, be their real self, stop making excuses. Inspiration sells without looking like a pitch. | Fits Personal Mission emails well |
| **Controversial** | Challenge a popular belief in the niche and give your real opinion. Gets attention; people who disagree still stick around. | E.g. challenge a popular manifestation dogma (visualisation alone, "just raise your vibration") — always land it back on the heart mechanism |
| **Challenge existing dogma** | Similar to controversial but aimed at "everyone does X, here's why that's wrong." | "Why most manifestation content skips the one thing that actually works" |
| **Bizarre/weird** | Off-the-wall angle that stands out purely by being unusual. Use sparingly — it's a spice, not a staple. | Use rarely; must still tie back to the argument, not just be weird for its own sake |
| **Current events** | Comment on something happening now (news, a holiday, a season) and tie it to the argument. | Seasonal tie-ins (New Year "predictions," a holiday) work well |
| **Charitable** | Tie a give-back moment into an email — genuinely good for goodwill and credibility. | Occasional, not a format to overuse |
| **Survey** | Ask the list a direct question ("reply and tell me...") — generates real language for future emails and shows what people actually want. | Good low-effort email when ideas are dry; also feeds `prospect_intel.md` |
| **"What it's not"** | List out what something ISN'T before revealing what it is — pure curiosity engine. | "It's not more visualising. It's not another meditation app. It's not..." |

**Never do:** Engagement-type emails don't map to this list — Engagement content doesn't adapt to email format.

### 8.3 The 13 Subject Line Techniques

Adapt every example — never copy Ben's wording or crude examples directly. Keep British English, no em-dashes, no clickbait beyond what actually pays off in the body.

1. **Shock effect** — a subject line that makes them go "whoa." Must be paid off immediately in the body copy, every time, no exceptions. Use sparingly. *Heart Creator angle: keep tasteful, no crude language, but a jarring reframe works.*
2. **Curiosity** — the single most powerful lever. People will pay just to satisfy curiosity. "My uncensored opinion on X," "The [X] I didn't expect," a glimpse into something personal.
3. **Straight benefit** — no cleverness, just the plain benefit. "How to feel your future as real," "Writer's block cured" style directness.
4. **Bizarre** — off-the-wall, tabloid-style oddity that still ties into the body. Use rarely, it's a spice not a staple.
5. **Play on popular quotes/phrases** — twist a known saying. Use phrases our audience would know (self-help/manifestation-world sayings, common idioms).
6. **Contrast** — put two words/ideas together that don't normally belong. "How to nab more results by doing less" style contradiction that makes the brain stop.
7. **Play on book/movie titles** — borrow a title people already recognise and twist a word. Use titles Rachel would know (self-help, spiritual, mainstream pop culture) rather than internet-marketing-world references.
8. **TV/movie characters** — reference a broadly known character. Must be very well known or it falls flat.
9. **Challenge existing dogma** — say the opposite of what "everyone knows." Must be genuinely defensible, not contrarian for its own sake.
10. **Numbers** — "5 ways," "7 signs," "3 things." People click on numbered promises almost reflexively.
11. **Authority and proof** — attach a credible name/source. For us: HeartMath Institute, real client names (with permission), genuine stats already in `model-for-health.md`.
12. **Seasonal** — tie to a real calendar moment (New Year, a season, a holiday). Gives a natural excuse to write and a natural reason for the reader to open.
13. **Mix and match** — combine two or more of the above in one subject line. The more that's mixed in, the more it stands out.

**Always pay off the subject line honestly in the body.** Never use shock/bizarre/curiosity that isn't actually addressed — that breaks trust fast.

### 8.4 Practical Mechanics

- **Length:** 200–300 words ideal, around 250 as the sweet spot — see Section 4.
- **Full URL vs hyperlinked text:** a plain full URL can outperform "click here" with older/less web-native audiences because it visually stands out. **This conflicts with our standing rule** (Section 5) that CTAs must always be inline hyperlinked text, never a plain URL. Our rule stays as-is — Olly confirmed this format previously — noted here only as a known alternative if we ever want to test it.
- **Variety over repetition** — never send the same content type or subject line technique twice in a row.
- **Idea sourcing — "everything is an email."** Anything that happens in daily life is a potential story or angle: a delivery running late, a mundane errand, a conversation. Nothing wasted ever happens to someone who writes regularly — it's all material once twisted toward the argument.
- **Frequency:** Ben mails daily and argues frequency builds a "must-read" reputation. Our cadence stays as-is per existing rules — this is a mechanic to know, not an instruction to increase send frequency without Olly's say-so.

### 8.5 What NOT to Import From This Source

- **No crude/raunchy language** — Ben's own examples get explicit at times. None of that belongs in Heart Creator's voice.
- **No "always be closing" aggression** — Ben's system leans hard-sell/urgency in places. Heart Creator's CTA tone rule (soft, conversational, never pushy, never "click here now") overrides this completely.
- **No unverified claims or fabricated authority** — any "proof" or "authority" subject line must use real Heart Creator facts (HeartMath research, real client names with permission) — never invent credibility.
- **No name-calling or mocking competitors** — Heart Creator's rule stands: never be dismissive of tools/teachers the audience has already tried — they worked partially, the point is what's missing, not that they or their teachers are wrong/stupid.

## 12. Belief Shift Email Technique

Use this structure for "Belief shift" type emails.

```
HOOK - THE BELIEF
State the belief directly in Rachel's own words, in quotation marks.
Name a specific technique she's tried - visualise, meditate, affirm - not "do the work" generically.
Good: "I just need to visualise better and be more consistent."
Note: Rachel may not be able to visualise at all - lead with effort and willpower, not visualisation.

IDENTIFIER
Acknowledge you hear this a lot. Name the context in 1-2 lines so she feels seen before the reframe.

VALIDATE
Meet her inside it. "If you've spent years trying things that haven't lasted, of course you'd feel that way."
Never make her feel foolish for holding the belief.

REFRAME - use questions, not declarations
"But what if..." questions introduce the reframe so she arrives at it herself.
Good: "But what if it's nothing to do with the technique? What if it's the STATE you're doing it in?"
Never state the reframe as a flat declaration.

The reframe itself should land as one clean logical sentence, not a science lecture:
"Your subconscious mind only responds to feeling."
Not: "Your nervous system is in high beta and blocking access to your subconscious mind."

SURVIVAL MODE - soften it
"It's just because you're still mainly operating from a survival-based state." Never clinical or blaming.

HEART LANGUAGE - not "Heart Coherence" at this stage
Use "opening your heart" / "getting into your heart" - warm, felt, accessible.

CLOSE - plant the seed
A forward-looking line rather than a hard CTA in early belief-shift emails.
"Getting out of your head and into your heart is where this all starts."
```

**Reframe pattern - the learning loop:** name the loop she's already stuck in - "So you end up having to go and learn more, to still not be able to really feel it. This pattern can go on for years." Works when the belief is about being stuck despite effort and knowledge.

**Promised land - be specific, not generic:**
Approved: "Your imagination feels like a creative tool rather than something you have to force."
Rejected: "You'll finally be able to create the life you want."

## 13. Personal Story Arc

Use for "Personal story" type emails (chronic illness thread or $13k conscious creation thread, or smaller everyday moments per Section 8.2).

```
HOOK - proof-first, not vague
Approved: "In November and December 2018... My income grew by 364%."
Rejected: "Back in 2018... Something shifted."

SETUP - 2-4 lines, concrete specifics
Ground it in real circumstances: housing, finances, a recent event (travel, injury, job loss).
"I was back living with my parents at 28. I'd just returned from travelling Europe.
I'd taken a serious head injury in September that was affecting my ability to work."

SYMPTOMS / WHAT IT LOOKED LIKE
5-8 specific, recognisable experiences - exhaustion, brain fog, the inspired state that
always crashes, the gap between knowing and feeling it. Don't lead with the diagnosis label.

TURNING POINT
"What I realised was..." / "Things only changed when..."

THE MECHANISM
Introduce Heart Coherence naturally, not as a pitch. Always "something called Heart Coherence" on first mention.

THE RESULT
Specific and grounded, not vague wellness promises.

CLOSE
CTA, soft sign-off, or open loop - see Section 5.
```
